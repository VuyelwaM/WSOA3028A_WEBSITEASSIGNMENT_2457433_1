Week 3/ Web Design Experience With UI and UX

The Norman Door I think this theory is a great way to introduce how user experience and user interface work in terms of any product really be it a door, a game, a website, and even clothing! It is important that the design of a product is not arbitrary and should always be clear as to what they do, when, and how, if a product fails to communicate that then it really is the designer’s fault and not yours as the user! This broadened my understanding of how important it is to remember as a designer and creator that what is in my head no one else can see or understand so I have to communicate clearly what my product or website is about and how it works, for example, if I have a button in my website, as per norm and learned convention the button should respond with whatever result I set it to. If the button does not interact in any way and it is just there for decoration, I have already failed as a designer and to please my client. Speaking of conventions, when I first engaged with Steven Mailloux reading about conventions and context I did not quite get how it was linked with UI and UX until some thorough thinking as to what exactly the text is about, Mailloux basically says that convention is a tradition passed down generation to generation or from academics to academics, these conventions are practiced because it is what people are used to and how we do things till today and that breaking these conventions could break a cycle and cause havoc, because now for example if a button does not have a function anymore then what does it do? And how will users get used to this invention? In the same breath, he does acknowledge that context can change, like the time and evolution of technology but the change of convention should be adjustable and understandable to all, like the book/paper hypermedia by Maria Fischer and Andrea Muller, these have been evolved from paper to digital screen, same convention but different context. Good UX and UI are when there is an interaction between the product and the user. We are provided with four definitions as to what interactivity is, Aaron Smuts says something is interactive if it is responsive if a user clicks on X at the top corner of a webpage the X should respond by taking the user to the homepage of the close page. Lev Monovish says that interactivity should not be random, if a user clicks on X it should not then take us to the next page or open a new website. Espen Aarseth and Gonzalo Frasca simply say that interactivity should be effortless/ easy.

Week 4/ Web Design Experience With Wireframes and UI

It is every web designer’s dream to create the coolest and most talked about website by having all cool animation effects and unending pop-ups, but it is all for nothing if the user does not know the purpose of your website or even knows how to use the interface. This week’s reading by UXPIN about the basic principles of user interface design made me realize the importance of planning before instilling in code, this planning process is basically drawing a blueprint on paper on how your website will look and work, it is also known as a wireframe. The wireframe will basically give you a guideline as to how the layout will look like and how it will work. This helps ensure that the design will be organized, intuitive and easy to use. By creating wireframes, designers can identify potential issues with the design early in the process. This saves time and money in the long run by preventing design flaws and usability problems that may arise later in the development process. Your design intention should speak for itself and be easy to understand, if your page needs the user to scroll up and down make sure you have something like a scroll bar or even signs that indicate up-and-down movement. Overall, wireframes are an essential part of the UI/UX design process. They help designers create a well-organized, user-friendly interface that meets the needs of both the user and the business.

Week 7/ Web Design User Experience with Marc Hassenzahl and Noam Tractinsky

This article got me thinking as an aspiring web designer that user experience is more than just animated scrolls, pop up-animations, and automatic text animation, it is about, and as mentioned in the article, beauty, aesthetics or art play an important role in the user interface, (“In the paper ‘Attention web designers: You have 50 milliseconds to make a good first impression!’ (this issue), Gitte Lindgaard, Gary Fernandez, Cathy Dudek, and Judy Brown show how fast impressions of the visual appeal of a website are formed and how stable they are.”) I don’t think the article is saying you should be an artist to have an effective user experience but make your webpage in a way that is appealing, use colours, be creative. The article also mentions how user experience is about emotion and effect. (“UX takes a ‘human’ perspective. It is interested in understanding the role of affect as an antecedent, a consequence and a mediator of technology use. In addition, it is rather focused on positive emotions.”) I believe a website acts or stands as the placeholder of viewing a store, portfolio or anything really virtually before stepping in physically, it is window shopping and taste of the actual product, so it is important that a website is easy to use, understand and navigate through as you would at a shop, if the user wants to look into high heels, is there a page for that in your store? And if they have further questions can they ask questions and receive an answer immediately as they would in store? These demands should be met to meet the total satisfactory of the user and have a positive user experience and not leave them frustrated waiting for the chat bot to reply or search bar unable to function because the website is only designed for computer and not mobile phone. The user will leave the website even if it has the likes Picasso artwork, if it does not meet their needs. Lastly, the article says user experience is about the experience. (“An experience is a unique combination of various elements, such as the product and internal states of the user (e.g. mood, expectations, active goals), which extends over time with a definitive beginning and end.”) How do you want the user to feel during browsing through your website? You could have a product they show no interest in but have the user spend almost an hour on your website, all because it gives them that excitement and feeling of being immersed by what is presented to them and interact with.
